# <center> Chapter 2 </center>

The second day of school arrived with a familiar, heavy silence. Damien navigated the hallways with practiced
anonymity, his eyes tracking the floor tiles as he moved. He preferred the morning's early hours, when the air
was still cool and the building hadn't yet been saturated with the loud, chaotic energy of the student body.
He liked the stillness; it made him feel less like an outsider and more like a quiet observer of a world that
didn't quite know what to do with him.

When he reached his classroom, he stopped. Someone was already sitting in the seat next to his—the one Dahlia
had vacated the previous day. It was a boy with slightly messy hair and thick-rimmed glasses, who was currently
propping his chin up with one hand while doodling on the corner of a notebook.

He looked up as Damien approached, his face brightening with a sudden, eager recognition. *"Hi! My name is Ben,"*
he said, his voice cutting through the quiet room. *"Remember me from high school? We were in the same history
class back in ninth grade."*

Damien paused, his hand gripping the strap of his bag. He searched his memory for a moment before nodding
slowly. *"Yeah, kinda. You sat near the window, right?"*

*"Spot on!"* Ben grinned, shifting in his seat to give Damien more room. *"You always seemed so quiet, like you
were in your own world. I was wondering... can I sit here? The glare from the window in my old spot is a
killer."*

Damien sat down, his movements careful and controlled. *"Yeah, sure. I don't mind."*

An awkward silence settled between them. Damien opened his bag and pulled out his own notebook, his fingers
tracing the edges of the paper. He didn't know what to say to fill the gap, and he wasn't sure if he wanted to.
Ben, however, seemed to have no such reservations.

*"So, do you live nearby?"* Ben asked, leaning in slightly. *"You're always at class so early. I barely beat
the janitor here today, and you were already halfway down the hall."*

Damien didn't look up from his desk. *"Yeah. It only takes about twenty minutes to walk."* He hesitated for a
heartbeat, then added, *"Five minutes if I fly."*

Ben’s eyebrows shot up behind his glasses. *"Wait, so you can fly?! That's cool! For real? Like, no tech or
anything?"* He leaned back, looking genuinely impressed. *"I live way across town, man. I mostly have to hitch
a ride with my dad. He drops me off on his way to work, which means I'm stuck here way before the first bell."*

Damien felt a small, rare tug of amusement at the corner of his lips. *"I was just joking, Ben. I can't fly."*

Ben blinked, then laughed, a self-deprecating sound. *"Yeah... yeah, I was also joking. Mostly. Though in this
city, you never really know, right?"*

The conversation was abruptly cut short as the biology professor marched into the room, his lab coat billowing
behind him. He slapped a stack of papers onto the podium and began a brisk lecture on human anatomy, his voice
a sharp contrast to the morning's earlier stillness.

Ben stayed in the seat next to Damien, though he was a restless student. He spent most of the lecture fidgeting
with his pen or staring out the window. At one point, he leaned over and whispered, his voice barely audible
over the professor's explanation of the circulatory system.

*"You know, it’s weird that I’m so interested in biology,"* Ben muttered, his eyes fixed on a diagram of a
heart on the chalkboard. *"I want to be a nurse, but damn, I’m absolutely terrified of blood. Do you think
that's a problem?"*

Damien didn't reply. He remained focused on the front of the room, his pen moving steadily across the page as
he transcribed the lecture. He felt Ben's gaze linger on him for a moment, waiting for a response that didn't
come, before the other boy finally turned back to his own notes.

By the time the class ended, Ben seemed to have reached a conclusion. He gathered his things with a thoughtful
expression. *"I think I might try the back row for the next period,"* he said, giving Damien a small, tentative
wave. *"See you around, Damien."*

Damien watched him go, feeling a strange mix of relief and a faint, unidentifiable pang of something else. He
was too serious, he knew. He was a statue in a world of movement, and most people eventually grew tired of
waiting for the stone to speak.

***

The third day of school brought a hazy, humid morning. Damien walked along the side of the highway, the steady
rhythm of his steps the only thing keeping him grounded. He scanned his surroundings, his eyes tracing the
distant silhouettes of students who were commuting in more... unconventional ways.

Above the tree line, he saw a few figures gliding through the air—some using sleek, expensive-looking
gravity-harnesses, while others moved with a more natural, fluid grace. A pair of students drifted past,
held aloft by their parents' invisible fields. It looked effortless, a way to bypass the clogged streets and
the mundane struggle of the walk.

Damien slowed his pace, his gaze drifting to his own hands. He felt the familiar hum of energy beneath his
skin, a potential waiting to be shaped. He looked around; the highway was busy, but the sidewalk was empty
of anyone who might recognize him. He could be at the school gates in a matter of seconds.

He began to focus, his fingers beginning to give off a faint, pearlescent shimmer—

*"Damien! Hey, Damien!"*

The shimmer vanished instantly as Damien spun around, his heart hammering against his ribs. A motorcycle had
pulled onto the shoulder just a few yards ahead of him. The rider kicked down the kickstand and pulled off
a black helmet, revealing Ben’s sweaty hair and lopsided grin.

*"Ben?"* Damien asked, his voice slightly breathless from the sudden surge of adrenaline.

*"The one and only!"* Ben shouted over the idling engine. *"What are you doing walking in this heat? Hop on,
man. I’ve got an extra helmet in the back."*

Damien looked at the motorcycle, then back at the empty stretch of road. The machine looked heavy and
unpredictable, but Ben’s invitation was genuine. *"I'm okay, Ben. I don't want to trouble you."*

*"Trouble? It's a straight shot to the school! Don't be a stranger, hop on!"*

Damien didn't know how to refuse without it feeling like a rejection. He took the helmet Ben handed him,
securing the strap with fumbling fingers, and climbed onto the seat. As Ben kicked the bike into gear, Damien
instinctively reached forward, his arms wrapping tightly around Ben’s waist in a sudden, panicked grip.

*"Dude!"* Ben laughed, his voice muffled by his own helmet. *"Is this your first time on a bike?"*

*"Yeah,"* Damien muttered, his eyes squeezed shut as the engine roared to life. *"Kinda."*

*"Just hold on, dude! I won't go fast, I promise!"*

Damien shifted his grip, finding the metal handles at the rear of the seat instead, his knuckles turning white.
The wind whipped past them, the roar of the highway becoming a dull hum in his ears. It was a different kind
of movement—loud, vibrating, and terrifyingly tethered to the asphalt.

*"So!"* Ben shouted, tilting his head back. *"Are you about to fly or something? Back there, I thought I saw
your hands glowing..."*

*"What?"* Damien yelled back. Between the roar of the wind and the padding of the helmet, Ben’s voice was a
distorted mess.

*"I said, are you about to—"*

*"What?"*

Ben shook his head, a gesture of defeat, and focused on navigating the traffic. He didn't try to speak again,
even after they pulled into the school parking lot and the engine finally died. Damien climbed off, his legs
feeling like jelly. He handed back the helmet, his expression carefully neutral, though his pulse was still
racing.

Ben gave him a thumb-up as he wheeled the bike into a space. *"See? Faster than walking!"*

Damien nodded, a small, genuine thank-you stuck in his throat. He had trusted someone else to get him where
he needed to go, and for once, the world didn't feel quite so transactional.

***

Dinner that evening was a quiet affair. Damien sat at the small kitchen table, the steam from the soup rising
between him and his mother. Away from the watchful eyes of his classmates, the guarded mask he wore at school
seemed to soften, his movements more relaxed, almost clingy in the way he stayed close to her as she served
the food.

*"How are your classmates, Damien?"* his mother asked, her voice warm and steady. *"Do they bother you? Is
there any bullying I should know about?"*

Damien looked up, a small smile playing on his lips. *"Mom, bullying isn't really a trend anymore. Besides,
everyone seems nice. Or at least, they leave me alone."*

*"Oh, that's good to hear. You should try to make some friends, you know. High school friends are different
from the ones you'll meet later in life. They're more... honest."*

Damien poked at his soup. *"I think I actually have one. Or at least, someone who talks to me a lot. His name
is Ben. He gave me a ride to school today on his motorcycle."*

His mother’s expression shifted instantly to one of sharp concern. *"A motorcycle? Damien, I told you those
things are dangerous! Are you sure your friend can drive that safely? You could have been hurt!"*

Damien felt a flicker of playfulness. *"Mom, I'm just joking. Why would I need to ride a motorcycle if I
can fly?"*

His mother froze, her eyes widening. *"Wait, did I not tell you to—"*

*"Mom, I’m just joking,"* Damien interrupted softly. *"There’s always a jeepney I can ride. You know that's
what I usually do."*

The sound of the front door opening cut through the room, followed by the heavy, measured footsteps of his
father. The atmosphere in the kitchen shifted instantly, the warmth evaporating into a stiff, formal silence.
His father entered the room, looking exhausted, his shoulders slumped from a long day of work. He gave a
curt nod to Damien before sitting down at the head of the table.

Damien and his mother shared a quick, silent look. Without a word, their conversation shifted, the spoken
words replaced by the clear, resonant hum of telepathy that only they could share.

*Okay, okay, next time avoid riding on something so dangerous—* his mother’s voice echoed in his mind, her
"tone" still laced with maternal worry.

*Mom, I said I was just joking,* Damien thought back, his gaze fixed on his plate.

*I’m just reminding you because I care. You're not a kid anymore, but—*

*I know, Mom. I'm not a kid. I know how to be careful.*

His father began to eat, the clink of his spoon against the bowl the only audible sound in the room. He didn't
look up, his presence heavy and imposing, a reminder of the distance that always seemed to exist between him
and his son.

*"No problems at school?"* his father asked after a few minutes, his voice gruff and devoid of the warmth
his mother possessed.

*"No,"* Damien replied shortly.

*"You don't need anything? Supplies? Money?"*

*"No."*

His father nodded once and continued eating in silence. Damien watched him for a moment, then turned back to
his mother, who gave him a small, encouraging smile. In the quiet of the house, surrounded by the unspoken
words and the weight of his father's expectations, Damien felt the strange, conflicting pull of his two
worlds—the one where he was a son, and the one where he was something else entirely.
